Case study

UK Domestic Real-Time A2A Merchant Payment Scheme

Payments · Real-time A2A · Faster Payments · Scheme Architecture

Architecture for a UK domestic real-time account-to-account (A2A) payment scheme that provides a card-like merchant and customer experience while settling instantly over Faster Payments and keeping control and economics within UK rails.

Context

UK domestic retail payments are dominated by global card networks, even though the UK operates a mature real-time account-to-account rail via Faster Payments (FPS). This creates higher domestic costs, longer settlement cycles and limited UK sovereignty over scheme rules and risk models.

The objective was to design a domestic A2A merchant payment scheme that could act as a modern alternative to cards for UK transactions: real-time settlement, lower cost, strong customer protection and a familiar experience for both merchants and customers.

Problem

The key challenges were:

  • Providing a card-like UX (tap, QR, online checkout) on top of FPS, which is natively “just a payment rail”.
  • Ensuring customer privacy by removing the need to expose bank account numbers to merchants.
  • Designing a central scheme layer that could coordinate routing, fraud, SCA and disputes for many issuers, acquirers and merchants.
  • Creating a clear liability and dispute model consistent with irrevocable FPS payments.
  • Maintaining international acceptance via co-badged instruments alongside global card networks.

Solution Overview

I designed a scheme-layer architecture organised into several domains, each responsible for a different part of the end-to-end A2A merchant payment lifecycle:

  • Customer domain – banking apps and wallets used for Strong Customer Authentication (SCA), approvals and dispute views.
  • Merchant domain – POS, SoftPOS and e-commerce platforms integrating via a single Merchant Integration Gateway.
  • Scheme access layer – standardised integration APIs for merchants, acquirers and issuers.
  • Scheme core – switching, orchestration, routing, tokenisation, fraud/risk and dispute management.
  • Issuer PSPs – customer ledgers, SCA and outbound FPS payments.
  • Acquirer PSPs – merchant settlement ledgers, merchant onboarding and inbound FPS processing.
  • Settlement rail – Faster Payments, used for real-time movement of funds between issuers and acquirers.

Key Architectural Components

1. Access Layer

  • Merchant Integration Gateway (MIG) – single northbound API for merchants and acquirers to initiate payments, query status, manage refunds and receive notifications. Abstracts underlying scheme complexity.
  • Issuer Integration Gateway – southbound API for issuers to receive payment requests, trigger SCA and return authorisation outcomes. Supports different integration patterns and secure, encrypted channels.

2. Scheme Core Services

  • Scheme Switch / Orchestrator – coordinates the end-to-end flow from merchant initiation to final confirmation, managing transaction state, applying scheme rules and calling risk services.
  • Tokenisation & Alias Service – maps QR/NFC tokens, device tokens or merchant-presented aliases to underlying customer accounts, ensuring account numbers are never exposed to merchants.
  • Directory / Participant Registry – maintains routing data, issuer and acquirer capabilities, limits and status; the switch uses this to choose the correct issuer/acquirer path.
  • Fraud & Risk Engine – performs real-time fraud scoring using behavioural analytics, device attributes, merchant profiles and transaction history. Provides allow/decline/step-up-SCA decisions.
  • Dispute Management & Liability Engine – manages claims and evidence, and assigns financial responsibility according to the scheme rulebook.
  • Refund Orchestration Engine – processes approved refunds as new FPS credit transfers back to the customer.
  • Data & Reporting Platform – centralised data store supporting clearing, scheme-level reporting, analytics, risk models and regulatory reporting.

3. Security & Platform Services

  • HSM-backed key management.
  • Mutual TLS and strong authentication between all scheme components.
  • Comprehensive audit logging and traceability across flows.
  • Operational monitoring, alerting and SRE practices.

End-to-End Payment Flow

  1. Merchant initiates payment: Merchant POS/SoftPOS/e-commerce integrates with the MIG to create a payment request.
  2. Scheme runs risk & routing: Scheme switch resolves customer token/alias, runs real-time fraud checks and selects the correct issuer/acquirer using the directory.
  3. Issuer SCA: The scheme sends an authorisation request to the issuer. The issuer triggers SCA in the customer’s banking app or wallet; the customer reviews and approves the payment.
  4. FPS settlement: Once approved, the issuer debits the customer and sends an FPS credit to the acquirer PSP.
  5. Merchant credited: The acquirer credits the merchant settlement account and updates merchant balances.
  6. Confirmation: Authorisation and settlement confirmations flow back through the scheme to the merchant, and the customer sees the transaction in their banking app in real time.

Disputes & Refunds Model

Because FPS payments are irrevocable, the scheme cannot rely on traditional chargebacks. Instead, it uses a claims-and-refunds model:

  1. Customer raises a claim with their issuer.
  2. Issuer submits the claim to the scheme dispute system.
  3. Merchant/acquirer provides supporting evidence.
  4. Liability Rules Engine determines which party is responsible.
  5. Refund Orchestration Engine instructs a new FPS credit from the liable party to the customer.

Optionally, a scheme guarantee pool can be introduced to handle specific categories of fraud (e.g. certain APP fraud scenarios), balancing customer protection with scheme economics.

International Payments via Co-Badging

For global acceptance, the architecture supports co-badged instruments where cards or wallet tokens carry:

  • A domestic A2A scheme identifier used by UK POS and merchants to route payments over the A2A scheme and FPS.
  • An international card network identifier (e.g. Visa/Mastercard) used abroad or where A2A is not supported.

This follows patterns similar to Brazil (Elo + Discover), Malaysia (MyDebit + Visa/Mastercard) and France (Carte Bancaire + Visa), keeping domestic transactions on low-cost national rails while maintaining global reach.

Outcomes

  • A coherent target architecture for a UK domestic A2A merchant payment scheme that can match card UX while using FPS for real-time settlement.
  • Strong customer privacy via tokenisation and issuer-based SCA.
  • Clear dispute and liability model aligned with FPS finality.
  • Architecture that balances domestic control, scheme economics and international acceptance through co-badging.
